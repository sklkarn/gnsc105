When a nucleus is formed, protons and neutrons come together and bind to form the nucleus. This process is driven by the strong nuclear force, which acts over short distances to hold the particles together. The formation of a nucleus also leads to the release of energy. This energy is in the form of electromagnetic radiation and called the binding energy. It is the same amount of energy required to separate the protons and neutrons in the nucleus when broken down to individual components. For example: The deuterium (\({}_{1}^{2}H\)) has one proton and one neutron in its nucleus. So when they break apart we expect the mass of deuterium atom equals the mass of one proton (i.e. \({}_{1}^{1}H\)) and the mass of one neutron. Mass of one \({}_{1}^{1}H = 1.0078 u \) and mass of one neutron = 1.0087 u. Therefore the total mass of
\begin{equation*} {}_{1}^{2}H = 1.0078 u+1.0087 u= 2.0165u \end{equation*}
 2  However, the measured mass of \({}_{1}^{2}H\) is only 2.0141 u, which is 0.0024 u less than the combined mass of proton and neutron. This loss of mass (also known as mass defect) appears in the form of binding energy of the deuterium atom. Similarly when one deuterium nucleus combines with one tritium nucleus to form helium nucleus tremendous amount of energy released which was a binding energy. For example:
\begin{equation*} {}_{1}^{2}H + {}_{1}^{3}H \rightarrow {}_{2}^{4}He + {}_{0}^{1}n +\text{energy}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Hence, the mass defect in this case is given by: total mass of
\begin{equation*} {}_{1}^{2}H + {}_{1}^{3}H = 2.0141u+3.0161 u= 5.0302u \end{equation*}
and total mass of
\begin{equation*} {}_{2}^{4}He + {}_{0}^{1}n = 4.0026u+1.0087 u= 5.0113u\text{.} \end{equation*}
Hence the difference in these masses is a mass defect, \(m = 0.0189u\text{.}\) This mass defect appears in the form of binding energy which can be calculated by equation (7.2.2)
\begin{align*} E \amp = mc^2 = 0.0189u\times (3.0\times 10^8 m/s)^2\\ E \amp =0.0189\times 1.67 \times 10^{-27} kg\times (3.0\times 10^8 m/s)^2 = 2.84\times10^{-12} J \end{align*}
Hence, energy released from this reaction is \(1.78\times 10^7 eV\) or, 17.8 MeV 4 .
